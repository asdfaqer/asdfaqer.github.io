Introduction

What is intelligence? This question has puzzled philosophers and scientists for centuries. Countless papers have been written off the back of this inquiry. One paper, "A Collection of Definitions of Intelligence", as the title suggest collects the countless definitions by Dictionarys, Phycologist, Pilosiphers, and countless others. The words R. J. Sternberg captures the ensence of the debate "Viewed narrowly, there seem to be almost as many deﬁnitions of intelligence as there were experts asked to deﬁne it." While there may be no consenus, the general direction of intelligence is generally agreed upon. One adaptivity, creatitivty, and wisdom all play a part in one intelligence. Naturally, one may ask themself, could human intelligence be replicated? Or even improved?


The Father of Computer Science

Perhaps one the most influential figures in the history of computer science is Alan Turing. In his 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," he asked the question can machines think? In which he proposed the imitation game, now widely known as the Turing Test. While he admitted that the test was not a proof of the preposition "machines can think", he argued that this new problem was closely related to the question of whether machines can think. If a machine could pass off as a human to an interrogator, then it would support the idea of machine being capable of thinking.
Over the years, many others have voiced similar thoughts. But with the advent of large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-5, Claude, and Gemini, this inquiry resurfaces with greater relevance. But new questions arise, how did we get here, and what are the challenges ahead?
